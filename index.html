01 · A true story

What three years of streets does to a brain

Your recorded introduction will play here

A few minutes, in your own voice — the story below, told the way you'd tell it.

To drive a black cab in London, you have to pass something called the Knowledge: roughly twenty-five thousand streets and thousands of landmarks, the whole tangled city held in your head, no satnav allowed. It takes most people three to four years, and over half never pass.

When researchers scanned qualified drivers' brains, a region tied to spatial memory — the posterior hippocampus — was measurably larger than in other people. The longer someone had driven, the larger it was. To rule out the idea that people with big hippocampi simply become drivers, the team followed trainees from their very first day: in the ones who eventually passed, that region grew over training. In the ones who didn't, it didn't.

The streets didn't just go into their memory. They reshaped the tissue doing the remembering — measurably, not metaphorically.

02 · The twist

Use it or lose it

It's tempting to read that as a self-improvement story: work hard, grow your brain, keep the prize. But the researchers also looked at older drivers who had stopped — drivers who'd once held all of London in their heads, then retired.

What do you think they found?

The drivers who had stopped no longer showed the enlargement. Once the daily demand was gone, the region drifted back toward a typical size. The growth was never a permanent trophy — it was the brain tracking what those drivers actually did, day after day. Stop doing it, and the brain quietly reclaims the space.

This is the real lesson, and it's bigger than taxis: your brain doesn't reward effort once and bank it. It continuously follows your life — in both directions.

04 · The idea

Same machinery. No two alike.

Every brain — yours, the taxi driver's, mine — runs on the same small set of universal parts and rules. That sameness is exactly why a brain can be studied at all: there are general principles to find. But run those same rules across one particular life — everything you've practised, feared, loved, repeated, and then stopped — and they build a brain that exists nowhere else.

Sameness of mechanism is what makes room for uniqueness. The rules are shared; what they're shaped into is yours, and it keeps changing.
